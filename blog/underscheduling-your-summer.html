The case for underscheduling your summer

What if the most intentional thing you did this season was leave space in it?

By Marsh & Muse · 5 min read

There is a particular kind of tiredness that arrives in June. Not the tiredness of having done too much — though that, too — but the tiredness of having planned too much. The summer mapped out before it has even begun. The weekends already spoken for. The good weather already earmarked.

We do it with the best of intentions. Summer is precious, we tell ourselves. It should be full. It should be memorable. And so we fill it — with trips and gatherings and commitments, with all the things we have been meaning to do — until the season itself starts to feel like another thing we have to get through.

Underscheduled is not lazy. It is not careless. It is, perhaps, the most deliberate choice you can make in a world that profits from your busyness.

The myth of the full life

Somewhere along the way, we started measuring the quality of our lives by their density. A full diary became shorthand for a full life. To be busy was to be valued. To have nothing planned on a Saturday felt wasteful — or worse, like evidence of something missing.

But density and richness are not the same thing. A day with nowhere to be, nothing to perform, no version of yourself to show up as — that day has texture too. It has the particular quality of light that only lands when you are not rushing through it.

What slow living has always understood is that presence is not a personality trait. It is a condition. And it requires space to exist.

What underscheduling actually looks like

This is not about cancelling everything. It is not a retreat or a radical overhaul. It is quieter than that, and more sustainable.

Underscheduling is the practice of deliberately leaving pockets of your time uncommitted. Not empty — just unassigned. A Sunday afternoon with no plan. A Tuesday evening with nowhere to be. A weekend that doesn't begin with a list.

In practice, it might look like:

  • Saying no to one thing this week that you would have said yes to out of obligation
  • Leaving at least one weekend day without a fixed plan
  • Protecting your mornings — even just the first hour — from other people's agendas
  • Resisting the urge to fill a cancelled commitment with a new one

Small things. Quietly radical.

· · ·

The nervous system case

There is a physiological argument here too. Our nervous systems were not built for the pace we are asking them to hold. Chronic overscheduling keeps the body in a low-level state of alertness — not quite stressed, but never quite at rest. The sort of background hum you stop noticing until it stops.

Real rest — the kind that restores rather than merely pauses — requires a nervous system that believes there is nothing it needs to be doing right now. That safety signal does not come from a holiday once a year. It comes from the ordinary days being a little less full.

When women describe what draws them to slow living, they rarely use the language of productivity. They say things like: I wanted to feel like myself again. I wanted to stop feeling behind. I wanted to actually be in my life, not just moving through it.

These are nervous system problems. And they have nervous system solutions.

Permission to leave it open

Here is what nobody tells you about underscheduling: the first few times you do it, it feels uncomfortable. The unplanned afternoon arrives and the old instinct reaches for something to fill it with. A task, a scroll, a plan. Anything to justify the time.

That discomfort is not a sign that you are doing it wrong. It is a sign that you have been doing it differently for a long time, and that something in you is learning a new thing.

Give it a moment. Let the afternoon be what it wants to be. Notice what surfaces when you stop managing every hour.

Often, it is something you had forgotten you enjoyed. A walk with no destination. A chapter read in good light. The particular pleasure of a cup of tea made slowly, drunk without looking at your phone.

· · ·

This summer, consider leaving some of it open. Not all of it — just enough. Enough that when something unexpectedly good arrives, there is room for it.

Underscheduled is not lazy. It is, in its quiet way, the most intentional thing you can be.
